Wave Division Multiplexing

Overview:
Wavelength Division Multiplexing (WDM) is an advanced optical communication technology used to transmit multiple data signals simultaneously through a single optical fiber using different wavelengths of light. This project presents the working principle, architecture, types, components, applications, advantages, challenges, and future scope of WDM systems.

Project Objectives:
Understand the fundamentals of WDM technology
Study optical fiber communication systems
Analyze CWDM and DWDM techniques
Learn the architecture and components of WDM systems
Explore real-world applications in modern communication networks


What is WDM?
WDM combines multiple optical carrier signals onto a single optical fiber by using different wavelengths (colors) of laser light.
This allows:
High bandwidth utilization
Faster data transmission
Reduced infrastructure cost
Long-distance communication

Working Principle:
Multiple optical signals are generated at different wavelengths.
A Multiplexer (MUX) combines these wavelengths.
Signals travel together through a single optical fiber.
A Demultiplexer (DEMUX) separates the wavelengths at the receiver side.
Optical receivers decode the transmitted data.

Advantages of WDM:
High bandwidth capacity
Efficient fiber utilization
Scalable communication system
Reduced operational cost
Supports long-distance communication

Limitations:
High installation cost
Complex system management
Precise wavelength control required
Optical interference issues
